# Research Decomposition: Dual-Board Bagchal (Devil's Plan S2 Variant)

## Source

- **Primary:** Self-contained Rust game engine in `crates/olympiad/games/bagchal/` — complete rules implementation with legal move enumeration, board outcome detection, and the dual-board mirror structure with first-to-resolve semantics.
- **Bots:** `crates/olympiad/bots/bagchal/` — `RandomBot` (uniform over legal moves) and `GreedyBot` (depth-1, `material_delta * 100 - opponent_mobility`, board-agnostic selection).
- **Domain:** Standard Bagchal is weakly solved as a draw (Lim et al., retrograde analysis). No published work addresses the Devil's Plan S2 dual-board mirror variant. The variant transforms a solved game into an open problem by adding board-selection as a strategic dimension.

## Sub-questions

### SQ1: Does board-selection policy measurably affect win rate?

**The core question.** The existing `GreedyBot` scores all legal moves (across both boards) with the same evaluation function and picks the highest. It never explicitly reasons about *which board to act on*. Does replacing this implicit board selection with an explicit policy — e.g., "always act on the board where you're most threatened" or "always push the board where you're closest to winning" — produce a statistically significant win-rate improvement?

**Operationalization:** Implement 4 fixed board-selection policies (always-attack, always-defend, material-responsive, mobility-responsive) as wrappers around the same underlying move evaluator. Measure pairwise win rates over 10,000 games each. The null hypothesis is that board selection doesn't matter — all policies perform within 2% of each other.

**Why this matters:** If the null holds, the dual-board variant is strategically equivalent to playing two independent single-board games. If it fails, board selection is a genuine strategic dimension unique to this variant.

### SQ2: Does the dual-board mirror structure eliminate first-mover advantage?

**Motivation:** In single-board Bagchal, goats move first and optimal play is a draw — but in practice, goat-first creates asymmetric dynamics (goats establish formations, tigers react). In the S2 variant, each player holds *both* roles. Seat 0 plays goats-left/tigers-right; seat 1 plays tigers-left/goats-right. Does this role mirroring neutralize the first-mover edge?

**Operationalization:** Measure seat-0 vs. seat-1 win rate across all bot matchup tiers (random-vs-random, greedy-vs-greedy, best-vs-best). If seat-0 win rate converges to 50% ± 1% across all tiers, symmetry is restored. If it deviates by ≥3%, the variant inherits or creates a first-mover advantage.

**Subtlety in the code:** The `settle_match` function (lib.rs:671) contains a tie-break rule — when both boards resolve on the same turn, the acting player's role determines the winner. Since the acting player alternates, this tie-break systematically favors one seat depending on when simultaneous resolution occurs (even vs. odd turn).

### SQ3: How coupled are the two boards during optimal play?

**Motivation:** If the two boards are strategically independent, the dual-board variant reduces to "play two single-board games and the faster one determines the winner." If they're coupled, cross-board reasoning is essential — e.g., sacrificing tempo on one board to gain it on another.

**Operationalization:** Measure mutual information between left-board and right-board states (captured goats, tiger mobility, goats placed) at game end. Compare against an independence baseline (shuffle board pairings across games). Also measure temporal coupling: does acting on board A on turn N predict which board is acted on at turn N+1?

**Why this is hard:** Coupling could be an emergent property that only appears at higher bot skill levels. Random bots will show zero coupling; greedy bots might show weak coupling; a minimax bot with cross-board evaluation might reveal strong coupling. This sub-question depends on SQ1's bots existing first.

### SQ4: What is the effective branching factor and game tree depth of the dual-board variant?

**Motivation:** The single-board Bagchal branching factor varies from ~5 (early tiger moves with few goats) to ~50+ (late-game goat slides). The dual-board variant roughly doubles the branching factor by offering moves on both boards. But the first-to-resolve stopping rule *shortens* games relative to two independent boards. How do these effects trade off?

**Operationalization:** Instrument the game engine to log branching factor (legal move count) and game length (turns to settlement) across thousands of games at each bot tier. Compare actual tree depth to single-board Bagchal benchmarks.

**Why it matters for bots:** If the effective depth is shallow (games resolve fast due to racing dynamics), shallow-search bots (depth 2-3) might be near-optimal. If games are long, deeper search or heuristic improvements dominate.

### SQ5: Does the stalemate rule (50-move no-progress counter) interact with board-selection strategy?

**Motivation:** The code (lib.rs:557-559) shows that the 50-move stalemate counter is per-board and favors goats. In the dual-board variant, a player could deliberately stall one board (as tiger) to trigger stalemate, which counts as a goat win — meaning the *opponent* wins that board. But is deliberate stalling ever rational? Or does the 50-move limit primarily function as a draw avoidance mechanism?

**Operationalization:** Measure stalemate frequency across bot tiers. If stalemates are rare (<1%), this sub-question is moot. If they're common, measure whether stalemate-aware board selection (avoiding moves that increment `no_progress_moves` on boards where you play tiger) improves win rate.

### SQ6: What evaluation function best captures cross-board state?

**Motivation:** The current `GreedyBot` scoring (`material * 100 - opponent_mobility`) is purely per-move — it evaluates the *resulting* board state after a candidate move but doesn't account for the *other* board's state. A cross-board evaluation might weight material differently depending on whether the player is winning or losing on the other board (e.g., "I'm ahead on the right board, so I should play conservatively on the left to avoid giving my opponent a faster resolution there").

**Operationalization:** Implement 3 evaluation function variants: (a) per-move only (current), (b) sum of both boards' scores, (c) board-differential-aware (scale aggression on one board by advantage on the other). Compare win rates.
